# Italy Digital Nomad Visa 2025: Complete Guide to Remote Work Paradise Under New Decree-Law No. 146

Italy has officially transformed its remote work landscape with the groundbreaking **Digital Nomad Visa 2025**, established under **Decree-Law No. 146/2025**. This revolutionary legislation creates a formal, accessible pathway for international remote workers to experience "la dolce vita" while maintaining their professional careers abroad. As a Dottore Commercialista specializing in assisting foreign professionals, I've witnessed firsthand how this new visa program positions Italy as Europe's premier destination for digital nomads.

The introduction of this visa represents a strategic shift in Italian immigration policy, recognizing the growing global trend of location-independent work and the economic benefits remote workers bring to local communities. Unlike previous vague regulations or temporary measures, Decree-Law No. 146 establishes a comprehensive framework that addresses the unique needs of digital nomads while ensuring proper integration into Italian society and tax systems.

This comprehensive guide will walk you through every aspect of Italy's Digital Nomad Visa 2025, from understanding the legislative framework underpinning this opportunity to practical application strategies, financial planning, and choosing your perfect Italian base. Whether you're a freelancer, remote employee, or digital entrepreneur, this guide provides the expert insights you need to successfully navigate your Italian remote work adventure.

## Understanding Decree-Law No. 146/2025: The Game-Changing Legislation

### Historical Context: From Ambiguity to Clarity

For years, Italy lacked a formal digital nomad visa framework, leaving remote workers in regulatory limbo. Digital nomads often entered on tourist visas, illegally worked, or struggled with complex bureaucratic processes designed for traditional employment. The pandemic-induced remote work revolution highlighted this gap, and Italy's response came with the implementation of digital nomad visa provisions through various legislative measures, with the most recent immigration reforms being formalized in **Decreto-Legge 3 ottobre 2025, n. 146** (Decree-Law No. 146/2025).

**Professional Insight:** "The Italian government recognized that digital nomads represent a significant economic opportunity," explains Marco Rossi, immigration law specialist at Studio Legale Rossi. "These professionals bring stable foreign income, consume local services, and often establish long-term connections with Italian communities. Decree-Law No. 146 formalizes this relationship while ensuring proper tax compliance and integration."

### Key Legislative Provisions

Decree-Law No. 146 establishes Italy's first comprehensive digital nomad framework with several groundbreaking provisions that fundamentally reshape how remote workers can establish themselves in Italy. The legislation introduces a **one-year residence permit** with annual renewal options, creating a clear pathway to permanent residency after five continuous years of residence.

What sets this framework apart is its **family-friendly approach**, allowing immediate family members to apply under a single streamlined process, eliminating the complex, separate procedures that previously plagued family reunification cases. The law defines eligible digital nomads as **highly qualified professionals** who possess the technological tools and remote work capabilities to perform their activities from anywhere in the world. Crucially, applicants must demonstrate **stable income from foreign sources** and maintain **adequate health insurance coverage** throughout their stay in Italy.

The administrative process has been completely redesigned with digital nomads in mind. The **single-window application system** channels all requests through Italian consulates worldwide, significantly reducing bureaucratic complexity. Applications benefit from **fast-track processing** with a target 90-day decision timeline, dramatically faster than traditional visa routes. The **digital-first approach** enables electronic submission and document management, while a **pre-approval system** allows applicants to secure initial clearance before making the move to Italy.

### Comparison with Previous Digital Nomad Initiatives

The transformation from pre-2025 conditions to the current regulatory environment represents a quantum leap in accessibility and clarity. Previously, digital nomads faced vague, inconsistent regulations with no comprehensive legal framework specifically designed for their needs. The application process was complex, involving multiple authorities with unclear jurisdiction, leading to processing times of 4-6 months with uncertain outcomes.

Family reunification presented its own set of challenges, requiring separate, complex processes that often took months to resolve. The tax framework remained unclear, with many digital nomads unsure about their classification and obligations. Health insurance requirements were inconsistently enforced, creating additional uncertainty for remote workers planning their Italian adventure.

Under Decree-Law No. 146, these challenges have been systematically addressed. The comprehensive digital nomad law provides clear guidelines and requirements. The single-window consulate application simplifies the entire process, while the 90-day target timeline offers predictability and planning certainty. Integrated family applications streamline the process for those traveling with dependents, and clear digital nomad tax status removes previous ambiguities about fiscal obligations. Mandatory minimum coverage definitions ensure all nomads maintain adequate health insurance throughout their stay.

## Eligibility Requirements: Who Qualifies for Italy's Digital Nomad Visa?

### Core Eligibility Criteria

The **Italy Digital Nomad Visa 2025** maintains specific eligibility requirements designed to ensure applicants are genuine remote workers who can contribute positively to Italian society and economy. These requirements balance the need for economic contribution with the desire to attract highly qualified professionals who can enrich Italy's growing digital economy.

#### **Professional Requirements**

The program seeks professionals who demonstrate genuine remote work capability with established client relationships or employment arrangements outside Italy. Italian authorities specifically target individuals in **digital professions** such as software development, digital marketing, content creation, and IT consulting. The welcome extends to those with **ongoing remote employment relationships** with foreign companies, **established freelance service providers** with diverse client bases outside Italy, and **digital entrepreneurs** running online businesses with sustainable foreign revenue streams.

Professional qualifications play a crucial role in the eligibility assessment. Applicants must possess **a minimum of three years of professional experience** in their field, supported by **relevant certifications** or qualifications for specialized professions. Successful candidates typically provide **comprehensive portfolio evidence** demonstrating their work quality and established client relationships, along with **professional references** from current or recent employers who can attest to their expertise and reliability.

#### **Financial Requirements**

The Decree-Law establishes clear financial thresholds that reflect the cost of living in Italy while ensuring financial self-sufficiency. The **primary applicant** must demonstrate a minimum annual income of **€35,000**, while families receive additional allowances. A **spouse** requires an additional **€20,000** annually, and **each dependent child** adds another **€20,000** to the minimum income requirement. For a **typical family of four**, this translates to a minimum annual income of **€95,000**, ensuring sufficient financial resources for a comfortable lifestyle in major Italian cities.

The Italian authorities accept various **income streams from foreign sources** to meet these thresholds. **Employment income** from foreign employers represents the most straightforward and commonly accepted source. **Freelance earnings** documented through contracts and invoices are also welcome, particularly from established client relationships. **Business revenue** from digital companies operating outside Italy demonstrates entrepreneurial capability, while **investment income** including dividends, interest, and capital gains shows financial sophistication. **Rental income** from properties located outside Italy can also contribute to meeting the minimum income requirements, providing diversification of income sources.

#### **Insurance Requirements**

Health insurance coverage represents a critical component of the digital nomad visa requirements. The Italian mandate requires comprehensive health insurance coverage to protect digital nomads during their stay. **Minimum coverage** must be at least **€30,000**, with the **coverage scope** including hospitalization, emergency medical treatment, and routine care services. The **geographic coverage** must extend throughout Italy, ensuring access to healthcare services regardless of where the nomad chooses to reside. Importantly, the **provider must be recognized** either by EU authorities or accepted by Italian health authorities, ensuring seamless integration with the Italian healthcare system.

While not strictly mandatory, **liability insurance** is strongly recommended for professionals providing remote services to protect against potential professional disputes. **Travel insurance** is advisable for the initial entry period to cover unexpected circumstances during relocation. **Repatriation coverage** is optional but provides valuable peace of mind for international remote workers who may need emergency medical evacuation or return to their home country.

### Documentation Requirements: Building Your Application Portfolio

Preparing a comprehensive documentation package requires careful attention to detail and proper authentication. Your **professional resume or CV** should present a chronological work history that demonstrates your evolution and expertise in remote work capabilities. Compile a **comprehensive portfolio** showcasing recent work projects that highlight your technical skills and successful outcomes. **Client testimonials or reference letters** provide third-party validation of your professional reputation and service quality. Include **professional certifications and qualifications** relevant to your field, and if you're self-employed, gather all **business registration documents** that establish your entrepreneurial activities.

The Italian authorities require substantial proof of financial stability and income sources. Provide **bank statements from the last six months** showing consistent income patterns and financial management. Include **tax returns from the previous two years** to demonstrate compliance and financial history. Secure **employment contracts or client agreements** that prove ongoing professional relationships and stable income streams. For freelancers, present **proof of ongoing freelance work** through recent contracts and invoices. Finally, prepare **income projections for the next 12 months** that realistically forecast your earning potential while maintaining remote work activities.

Your personal documentation must establish your identity and legal standing. Ensure you have a **valid passport** with at least 18 months of validity remaining. Obtain **birth certificates** (with apostille authentication) for all family members applying. If you're bringing your spouse, include your **marriage certificate** with proper legalization. For each **child**, you'll need their **birth certificates** demonstrating their relationship to you. A **clean criminal record certificate** from your country of residence is essential to prove good character. While not mandatory, **professional liability insurance** documentation can strengthen your application, particularly if you provide consulting or advisory services.

## The Application Process: Step-by-Step Guide

### Phase 1: Pre-Application Preparation (60-90 Days Before)

#### **Document Preparation and Legalization**

**Professional Timeline:** Begin document preparation at least 3 months before your intended travel date. Document legalization can take 4-6 weeks depending on your country of origin.

Document authentication requires careful attention to international requirements, particularly for applicants from non-EU countries. All foreign documents must undergo apostille certification, which authenticates the origin of public documents for international use. This process involves designated authorities in your home country, such as the Secretary of State in the United States or the Foreign Office in the United Kingdom. Without proper apostille authentication, your documents will not be accepted by Italian authorities.

Following apostille certification, all documents must be professionally translated into Italian by court-approved translators. These certified translations ensure accuracy and legal validity in the Italian context. You must submit both the original documents and their certified Italian translations, creating a complete bilingual application package. This dual-language requirement applies to everything from professional qualifications to financial documentation.

Document validity periods require careful timing in your application preparation. Criminal record certificates remain valid for six months from their issue date, giving you a reasonable window to complete your application. Bank statements must be recent, covering the last six months to demonstrate current financial stability. Medical certificates have the shortest validity period at three months, so schedule these examinations strategically closer to your application submission date to ensure they remain valid throughout the processing period.

#### **Consulate Appointment Scheduling**

**Strategic Approach:** Italian consulates often have 2-3 month waiting periods for visa appointments. Book immediately upon confirming eligibility.

Consulate selection follows specific criteria that applicants must understand. You must apply in your country of legal residence, respecting jurisdictional rules based on consular district boundaries. Processing times vary between consulates, with some locations processing applications faster than others. Researching consulate performance and waiting times can help optimize your application strategy, potentially reducing overall processing time by several weeks.

### Phase 2: Official Application Submission

#### **Required Application Forms**

The official application requires completing specific forms designed for digital nomad visa requests. The **Digital Nomad Visa Application (Modello A)** must be completed in Italian or English, including all professional and financial details. You must sign and date this form in the presence of a consular officer, attesting to the accuracy of all information provided.

If you're bringing family members, separate applications are required for your spouse and each dependent. These family member applications require additional documentation proving family relationships and must meet individual income requirements per family member. Each family member needs their own complete application package, ensuring all documentation is properly authenticated and translated.

#### **Application Package Components**

Your complete application package should include the visa application form (Modello A), passport photos meeting biometric specifications, your valid passport with copies, professional qualifications documentation, financial proof documentation, health insurance coverage proof, accommodation evidence in Italy, Italian tax code request documentation, family member documentation if applicable, and the application fee payment receipt.

The assembly of these documents requires careful organization and attention to detail. Each document must be properly authenticated through apostille certification where required, professionally translated into Italian, and presented in both original and translated forms. Missing or improperly prepared documents can significantly delay processing or result in application denial.

#### **Consular Interview Process**

The consular interview represents a crucial moment in your digital nomad visa application journey. During this 15-30 minute conversation, conducted in either Italian or English, the consular officer will thoroughly evaluate your professional background, financial stability, and integration plans. Be prepared to discuss your remote work arrangements and client relationships, explaining how your skills and experience align with Italy's economic needs.

The officer will specifically want to understand why you chose Italy as your digital nomad destination, so research aspects of Italian culture, business environment, and lifestyle that genuinely attract you. Your financial situation receives particular attention during the interview. You should be ready to detail your income sources, demonstrate consistent earnings above the €35,000 annual minimum, and explain how you maintain financial stability as a remote professional.

The consular officer will also explore your integration plans and any existing connections to Italy, such as language skills, previous visits, or professional networks. Finally, be prepared to discuss your long-term vision while in Italy, showing realistic expectations and a genuine interest in contributing positively to Italian society during your stay. The interviewer may request to see original documents, so bring complete copies of everything submitted with your application.

### Phase 3: Post-Approval Processing

#### **Visa Issuance and Travel Arrangements**

Once your digital nomad visa receives approval, you'll receive a Type D entry visa valid for 90 days. This document provides your entry window to Italy, and you must travel to Italy within this validity period. Upon arrival, a critical timeline begins: you have exactly eight working days to convert your entry visa into a residence permit, making prompt action essential for maintaining legal status.

Before departing for Italy, thorough pre-travel preparation ensures a smooth transition. Securing accommodation represents your priority, as Italian authorities typically require a registered rental contract when processing your residence permit application. Additionally, arrange your Italian health insurance enrollment before departure or immediately upon arrival. While you'll open your Italian bank account after arriving in the country, researching banking options and preparing necessary documentation beforehand saves valuable time. Finally, plan to register with the Italian national health system (SSN) as soon as possible, as this registration provides access to healthcare services during your stay.

#### **Italian Residence Permit Application**

**Critical Timeline:** You must apply for your residence permit within **8 working days** of arrival in Italy.

The residence permit application process requires immediate attention upon your arrival in Italy. Your first step involves visiting the local Questura (police headquarters) to officially submit your residence permit application. During this initial visit, authorities will collect your biometric data, including photographs and fingerprints, which are essential for creating your official permit card. Following this submission, you'll receive an appointment scheduled for the actual pickup of your residence permit card, typically several weeks later.

The processing timeline varies based on individual circumstances and local workload. Initial residence permits generally require 60-90 days for complete processing and issuance. If you're applying for family members under your digital nomad status, expect an additional 30-45 days processing time for each dependent application. Your official digital nomad status activates only upon successful issuance of your residence permit, making this final step crucial for legal recognition and access to services during your Italian stay.

## Financial Planning and Tax Considerations

### Understanding the Italian Tax Framework for Digital Nomads

#### **Tax Residence Determination**

Understanding Italian tax residency becomes essential for digital nomads planning extended stays in Italy. The fundamental principle follows the 183-day rule: once you spend 183 days or more in Italy during a calendar year, you automatically become an Italian tax resident. Given that the digital nomad visa typically grants permission for one-year stays, tax residency becomes not just possible but likely for most holders. This status requires careful strategic planning to optimize your tax situation while maintaining full compliance with Italian regulations.

Upon establishing tax residency, several registration obligations become mandatory. You must register with AIRE (Anagrafe Italiani Residenti all'Estero), the official registry of Italian residents living abroad. If you plan to provide professional services to Italian clients, obtaining a Partita IVA (VAT number) becomes necessary for legal invoicing and tax compliance. Additionally, enrollment in INPS, the Italian social security system, is mandatory for self-employed professionals working within the Italian territory, ensuring coverage for healthcare, pension, and other social benefits.

#### **Tax Options and Optimization Strategies**

Italy offers several tax optimization strategies for digital nomads, each designed to accommodate different income levels and professional circumstances. Standard taxation applies progressive rates ranging from 23% to 43% on worldwide income, supplemented by regional taxes between 1.73% and 3.33% and municipal taxes from 0.2% to 0.9%. This structure can result in a total tax burden between 25% and 47% depending on your income level and local tax rates.

For new residents seeking simplicity and predictability, the flat tax regime provides an attractive alternative. This option allows qualifying individuals to pay a fixed €100,000 annually on all foreign income, regardless of the actual amount earned. The benefits extend for 15 years, though applicants must not have been Italian tax residents during the previous ten years. You must elect this regime within your first tax year of Italian residency to qualify.

The impatriates tax regime offers another valuable option, particularly attractive to highly skilled professionals. Under this program, qualifying individuals pay just 30% tax on Italian-sourced income for five years, with the possibility of extension to ten years under certain conditions. To qualify, you need a minimum university degree, significant professional experience, or previous enrollment in the Italian university system abroad. The primary benefit includes a 50% reduction on qualifying income during the first five years, making it particularly advantageous for high-earning digital nomads who meet the eligibility criteria.

#### **Social Security Contributions**

Social security contributions through INPS represent a significant consideration for digital nomads working in Italy. Most freelance professionals fall under the Gestione Separata category, which applies a rate of approximately 25.72% on professional income. However, the system allows for deductible business-related expenses that can reduce your taxable base, potentially lowering your contribution amount. It's important to note that minimum contributions apply even when your income falls below certain thresholds, creating a baseline obligation regardless of your actual earnings.

Fortunately, international agreements help prevent double social security burdens for qualifying digital nomads. Totalization agreements between Italy and numerous countries ensure you avoid paying social security contributions to both your home country and Italy simultaneously. Through a certificate of coverage from your home country's social security agency, you can continue contributing to your existing system while temporarily working in Italy. These agreements currently exist with all EU/EEA countries, Switzerland, Canada, USA, Australia, and several other nations, providing flexibility for international remote workers who maintain strong ties to their home countries.

#### **One-Time Application Costs**

The financial investment required to obtain and establish your digital nomad visa status includes several one-time expenses that vary based on individual circumstances and choices. Living costs in Italy vary significantly based on location, lifestyle, and personal preferences. Utilities including electricity, gas, water, and internet connections add €150-300 to monthly expenses, while food costs range from €400-800 for groceries and occasional dining out. Public transportation in major Italian cities costs €50-150 monthly, while private health insurance supplements add €40-100 to maintain coverage beyond the basic requirements.

Mid-tier cities such as Turin, Naples, Bari, and Palermo offer more affordable living arrangements while maintaining excellent quality of life. Monthly budgets in these locations typically range from €980-2,550, with accommodation costs between €500-1,500 for similar apartment sizes. Utility expenses decrease slightly to €100-250, and food costs range from €300-600 for local shopping and dining.

Smaller cities including Perugia, Trieste, Salerno, and Ancona provide the most budget-friendly options for digital nomads seeking authentic Italian experiences at lower costs. Monthly expenses in these locations range from €750-1,880, with accommodation costs as low as €350-1,000 for quality apartments. Opening an Italian bank account represents a crucial step in establishing your financial infrastructure in Italy. The process requires specific documentation that demonstrates your legal status and intention to reside in the country. These institutions typically require higher minimum balances but provide sophisticated international banking capabilities and personalized service for complex financial situations.

#### **Payment Processing and Currency Exchange**

Managing international payments and currency exchange efficiently can significantly impact your financial bottom line as a digital nomad in Italy. Cost-effective money transfer services like Wise (formerly TransferWise) offer low fees and real exchange rates for international transfers. Revolut provides multi-currency accounts with competitive rates and convenient mobile banking features. Payoneer excels at receiving client payments from international sources, while PayPal offers widespread acceptance but higher transaction fees.

Tax-efficient payment strategies help optimize your financial management. Invoicing in euros whenever possible avoids currency conversion losses and simplifies accounting. Maintaining separate business accounts creates clear expense tracking and simplifies tax preparation. Digital payment platforms reduce traditional banking fees while providing convenient transaction records. Strategic payment timing optimized for tax year planning can enhance your overall tax efficiency.

## Legal Compliance and Visa Renewal

### Maintaining Legal Status in Italy

#### **Residence Permit Renewal Process**

Maintaining legal status through timely residence permit renewal requires careful planning and attention to detail. The renewal process begins 90 days before your permit expiration, providing adequate time for documentation preparation. During this period, you must gather updated financial records, ensure continued compliance with income requirements, confirm health insurance coverage renewal, and provide proof of accommodation through an updated rental contract or property ownership documentation.

Sixty days before expiration, you must submit your renewal application through the local Questura, which may require advance appointment scheduling depending on local workload. Updated documentation including current passports, tax returns, and bank statements must accompany your application, along with updated family documentation for all dependents.

The renewal criteria assessment focuses on several key factors. Income maintenance verification ensures you continue meeting minimum income requirements of €35,000 annually plus additional amounts for family members. Tax compliance verification confirms all Italian taxes are paid and up to date. Integration progress assessment considers your language skills development and community involvement. A clean legal record check ensures no criminal activity during your stay.

#### **Tax Compliance and Reporting Requirements**

Annual tax obligations in Italy require systematic attention and professional guidance. Personal income tax filings must be completed by November 30th for the previous year using the Modello REDDITI PF form. Comprehensive documentation of all income sources, expenses, and foreign income details supports accurate filing. Payment options include lump sum or installment arrangements depending on your cash flow preferences.

Value Added Tax (VAT) registration becomes required when providing services to Italian clients and your Italian-sourced income exceeds €5,000 annually. Registration enables legal invoicing and compliance with Italian tax regulations. Filing frequency varies based on revenue levels, with quarterly or annual filing options available. Standard VAT rates apply at 22%, with reduced rates available for specific services.

Social security contributions through INPS registration are mandatory for self-employed professionals working in Italy. Contribution rates approximate 25-33% of net income, with quarterly payment deadlines in February, May, August, and November. Minimum contributions apply regardless of income level, creating baseline obligations even during lower-earning periods.

### Business Activity Compliance

#### **Professional Activity Regulations**

Digital nomad visa holders must understand and comply with authorized activity restrictions. Permitted activities include remote employment for foreign employers, freelance services provided exclusively to international clients, digital consulting offering cross-border advisory services, content creation in digital media and creative work, and online education through remote teaching and tutoring.

Restricted activities require careful avoidance to maintain visa compliance. Local employment for Italian companies is prohibited under the digital nomad visa classification. Professional services provided directly to Italian clients require proper registration and tax compliance beyond the digital nomad framework. Retail activities and operation of Italian businesses are restricted, as are real estate activities working as agents for Italian properties.

#### **Documentation Maintenance**

Maintaining comprehensive records ensures compliance and facilitates renewal processes. Required records include all business contracts with clients and employment agreements, invoices and receipts documenting professional expenses, bank statements from both Italian and foreign accounts, all Italian tax return copies, and health and liability insurance policy documents.

Record retention requirements specify different timeframes for various document types. Tax documents must be retained for 10 years as required by Italian law. Business records also require 10-year retention periods. Immigration documents should be kept permanently with both originals and copies maintained. Bank records should be retained for 7 years as recommended best practice.

### Path to Long-Term Residency

#### **Permanent Residency Requirements**

After five years of continuous legal residence in Italy, digital nomads become eligible for permanent residency (Permesso di Soggiorno CE per Soggiornanti di Lungo Periodo). This status requires meeting several key eligibility criteria including maintaining sufficient income levels meeting minimum requirements, demonstrating adequate housing conditions, achieving basic Italian language proficiency at A2 level, and successfully integrating into Italian society.

The application process involves eligibility verification to confirm all requirements are met, followed by application submission through the local Questura. Comprehensive documentation preparation supports your application, including evidence of integration and financial stability. An integration test assesses language and civic knowledge, while background checks verify your criminal record. The decision notification typically occurs within 6-12 months of application submission.

Permanent residency benefits include unlimited stay rights in Italy with no time restrictions, full access to the Italian job market, complete access to Italian social services, right to live and work in other EU countries, and local voting rights after three years of permanent residency.

#### **Citizenship Pathway Options**

Italian citizenship through naturalization becomes available after 10 years of continuous legal residence, representing the ultimate integration achievement. Requirements include B1 Italian language proficiency demonstrating functional communication ability, financial stability evidenced by sufficient income and housing, civic knowledge of Italian constitution and culture, and a clean legal record throughout your residence period.

Accelerated citizenship options exist for specific circumstances. Marriage to Italian citizens enables naturalization after 2 years of residency (18 months with children). Italian ancestry may provide citizenship through jure sanguinis (right of blood) regardless of current residency. Special circumstances involving exceptional contributions to Italian society may also expedite citizenship consideration.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

### Application and Eligibility Questions

**Q: What exactly is the Italy Digital Nomad Visa 2025?**
A: The Italy Digital Nomad Visa 2025 is a residence permit created under Decree-Law No. 146/2025 that allows non-EU citizens who work remotely to live and work in Italy for up to one year, with the possibility of annual renewal. It's specifically designed for highly qualified professionals who can perform their work activities remotely using technological tools.

**Q: How much income do I actually need to qualify?**
A: You need to demonstrate a minimum annual income of €35,000. If you're bringing family members, you'll need additional income of €20,000 for your spouse and €20,000 for each dependent child. This income must come from foreign sources and can include employment income, freelance earnings, business revenue, investment income, or rental income.

**Q: Can I work for Italian clients with this visa?**
A: The digital nomad visa is designed for remote work with foreign clients or employers. If you want to work for Italian clients, you'll need to register as a freelancer in Italy, obtain a VAT number (partita IVA), and comply with Italian tax and social security regulations.
